Legislative History

Maryland's autonomous vehicle regulatory timeline across two sessions

2017
First AV bills introduced; not reported favorably
HB 2013 and SB 902, which would have established an AV testing and permitting framework, were reported unfavorably by their respective transportation committees. Maryland's first attempt to establish autonomous vehicle policy did not advance.
Dec 2020
Gov. Hogan releases Maryland CAV Strategic Framework
Following five years of work by the MDOT Connected and Automated Vehicles Working Group — established in 2015 — Gov. Hogan releases a statewide strategic framework for AV deployment. The document, developed with input from over 600 stakeholders, identifies five focus areas: public education, planning and policy, early deployment and testing, infrastructure, and workforce development. The framework signals Maryland's intent to welcome AV technology but does not create a statutory deployment pathway.
Feb 7, 2025
HB 1256 and SB 949 introduced
Del. Ziegler introduces HB 1256; Sen. Love cross-files SB 949. Both bills would establish operational standards for fully autonomous vehicles on Maryland highways, including law enforcement interaction protocols, and would ensure consistent statewide standards in place of a potential patchwork of local rules.
Feb 27, 2025
House Environment and Transportation Committee holds hearing on HB 1256
The committee received public testimony on HB 1256. No further committee action followed. SB 949 was similarly heard but not advanced in the Senate Judicial Proceedings Committee. Neither bill received a floor vote.
Apr 7, 2025
2025 session adjourns sine die. HB 1256 and SB 949 expire.
The General Assembly concludes its 90-day session. Both AV enabling bills expire without receiving floor votes in either chamber. Maryland enters the interim period without an autonomous vehicle deployment framework in statute.
Delay clock begins
Jan 14, 2026
2026 session convenes. SB 909 and HB 1295 introduced.
Sen. Love introduces SB 909 and Del. Ziegler introduces HB 1295, legislation substantially similar to the prior session's bills. The bills would establish AV operational standards, set minimum insurance requirements, require first responder interaction protocols, and apply the Online Data Privacy Act to AV-collected data.

Mar 4–5, 2026
Committee hearings held on SB 909 and HB 1295
The Senate Judicial Proceedings Committee and House Environment and Transportation Committee each conducted hearings. John Seng of Safe Roads Maryland testified that autonomous technology addresses the human error that underlies most road fatalities. The R Street Institute submitted written support, citing research associating AVs with 62% fewer police-reported crashes compared to human drivers. Senators raised technical questions regarding implementation but did not express substantive objection to the bills' framework.
Mar 17–23, 2026
Bills do not advance past committee reporting and crossover deadlines
Under General Assembly practice, bills with legislative support are expected to clear committee by around the 63rd day of session (March 17) and cross to the opposite chamber by approximately the 69th day (March 23). Neither SB 909 nor HB 1295 was reported out of its originating committee by these dates, substantially reducing the likelihood of enactment in the 2026 session.
Apr 13, 2026
2026 session adjourns sine die. SB 909 and HB 1295 expire.
The 2026 General Assembly session concludes its 90-day calendar. Neither autonomous vehicle bill was brought to a floor vote. Maryland remains without a statutory framework for autonomous vehicle operation. Legislation may be reintroduced when the General Assembly next convenes in January 2027.

Methodology

Data sources, assumptions, and important limitations

How the counter works

The counter estimates the potential safety benefit foregone since April 7, 2025, when the 2025 General Assembly session adjourned without advancing HB 1256 or SB 949. Maryland fatality data used: 2023 (621, MDOT), 2024 (582, Gov. Moore preliminary), 2025 (480, Gov. Moore preliminary). Each year's rate is used to interpolate a daily figure, which is then multiplied by the 85% crash-reduction factor at a 1.5% effective VMT share.

Estimate methodology

The headline figure applies an 85% any-injury crash reduction to 1.5% of vehicle miles traveled. The 85% figure is drawn from Kusano et al. (2024), which analyzed 7.1M Waymo miles. A 2025 follow-up study covering 56.7M miles confirmed 85% fewer suspected serious injury crashes. The 1.5% VMT share is derived by multiplying Maryland's estimated ride-hail share of city-core VMT (~6.9%, Fehr & Peers 2019) by Waymo's observed rideshare market share in San Francisco (~22% by end of 2024). Annual estimate: 480 deaths × 0.85 × 0.015 ≈ 6.1 preventable deaths per year.

Key assumptions and limitations

  • The counter begins at the 2025 session sine die (April 7, 2025). A reasonable alternative starting point is the 2026 sine die (April 13, 2026), reflecting only the most recent session's inaction.
  • The 85% crash reduction reflects Waymo's operational experience in Phoenix and San Francisco. Outcomes in Maryland may differ given differences in road conditions, traffic patterns, and urban density.
  • The fatality-specific reduction is extrapolated from injury crash data. The effect on fatal crashes specifically may be higher or lower than the overall injury crash reduction.
  • The 1.5% effective VMT share is a modeled estimate. No AV ride-hail service currently operates in Maryland.
  • These figures represent a modeled potential, not a forecast. Actual outcomes would depend on fleet size, deployment geography, vehicle mix, and operational parameters.
  • Maryland's 2025 fatality decline of approximately 18% may reflect multiple contributing factors and should not be attributed to any single cause.
