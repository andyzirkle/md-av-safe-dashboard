The Delay

Maryland's autonomous vehicle regulatory history

2017
First AV bills die in committee
HB 2013 and SB 902, twin bills to establish an AV testing framework, were reported unfavorably by transportation committees. Maryland's first attempt at AV regulation failed.
Feb 7, 2025
HB 1256 / SB 949 introduced: AV enabling bills
Del. Ziegler introduces HB 1256; Sen. Love cross-files SB 949. Both bills would establish standards and requirements for fully autonomous vehicles on Maryland highways, and prohibit localities from banning them. A hearing is scheduled for February 27.
Feb 27, 2025
HB 1256 heard in committee, then silence
The House Environment and Transportation Committee holds a hearing on HB 1256. The bill receives no further action: no vote, no floor consideration. It dies without advancing out of committee. SB 949 meets the same fate in the Senate.
Apr 7, 2025 · Delay Clock Starts
2025 session adjourns sine die. HB 1256 / SB 949 dead.
The Maryland General Assembly adjourns at midnight. AV enabling legislation that was introduced, heard, and ignored dies with the session. Maryland remains without an AV framework despite having had a clear legislative vehicle to pass one.

2026 session opens. New bills, same story.
SB 909 (Sen. Love) and HB 1295 (Del. Ziegler) are introduced, nearly identical to the bills that died in 2025. More than half of U.S. states have already passed AV enabling frameworks. Maryland has none.

Hearings held. Supporters testify. No vote scheduled.
The Judicial Proceedings Committee hears SB 909; the Environment and Transportation Committee hears HB 1295. John Seng of Safe Roads Maryland testifies that driverless services remove the most dangerous element of driving: the human. Methodology & Sources

Full transparency on data, assumptions, and limitations

How the counter works

The live counter estimates preventable deaths by interpolating Maryland's cumulative traffic fatalities since April 7, 2025 — the moment the 2025 General Assembly adjourned sine die, killing HB 1256 and SB 949 without a vote despite having held hearings on both. Maryland fatality data: 2023 (621 deaths), 2024 (582 deaths), 2025 (480 deaths, preliminary from Gov. Moore's office). The counter runs through the 2025 remainder at the 2025 rate (480/yr) and continues into 2026 at the same projected pace. Deaths are interpolated linearly within each year.

Estimate methodology

The headline figure applies an 85% any-injury-reported crash reduction to 1.5% of vehicle miles traveled. The 85% comes from Kusano et al. (2024), which analyzed 7.1M rider-only miles. A subsequent 2025 study covering 56.7M miles found 85% fewer suspected serious injury+ crashes. The 1.5% effective VMT share multiplies Maryland ride-hail's estimated share of city-core VMT (~6.9%) by benchmark Waymo rideshare penetration in San Francisco (~22% by end of 2024). Annual preventable deaths = 480 × 0.85 × 0.015 ≈ 6.1/year ≈ 0.0168/day.

Key assumptions & limitations

  • The delay clock starts at the 2025 session sine die (April 7, 2025) — the first moment Maryland definitively failed to act on enabling legislation it had heard in committee. One could start the clock even earlier, at the Feb 27, 2025 committee hearing where HB 1256 stalled.
  • The 85% reduction comes from Kusano et al.'s analysis in Phoenix and San Francisco. Maryland's road conditions and driver behavior differ.
  • Fatality reductions are extrapolated from injury crash data. Fatality-specific reductions may be higher or lower.
  • The 1.5% effective VMT share is speculative — no Waymo service currently operates in Maryland.
  • These figures represent potential lives saved, not certainties. Real-world results depend on deployment specifics.
  • Maryland's 2025 fatality drop (−18%) may reflect improved safety trends independent of AV technology.
  • The delay clock starts at the 2026 session open; one could argue a longer delay going back to prior sessions.
